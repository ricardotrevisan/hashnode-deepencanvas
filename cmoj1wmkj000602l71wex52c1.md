### There’s a familiar, daily pattern: you start noticing the campaign — a new product, a solution that will change everything, a better way to do something you already do. At first it sounds like background noise. Then it grows: experts show up with data, influencers with testimonials, infographics that explain why you hadn’t yet realized how much you needed it. The offer becomes multifront, insistent, almost omnipresent. And then you buy.

And the thing disappears.

Not the product — that stays on the shelf, in your home, on your phone. What disappears is the conversation. Experts migrate to another topic. Influencers are onto something else. Infographics vanish. The product that weeks ago was described as *life-changing* no longer appears anywhere, because now there’s another occupying that space. Those with short memories don’t notice the substitution. Those with long memories start asking uncomfortable questions.

---

The most obvious question is: why does marketing vanish right after the purchase?

The answer is less cynical than it seems, but no less disturbing. Modern marketing doesn’t sell a product — it sells a state of anticipation. And anticipation, by definition, collapses the moment the object lands in your hands. The perceived value of anything is highest before the purchase, while it’s still projection, possibility, promise. Afterward it becomes reality — with friction, with limitations, with the distance between what was promised and what is delivered. The job of marketing is to maximize that pre-purchase peak and sustain it long enough to trigger the transaction. What comes after isn’t the marketing team’s problem. The product has already fulfilled its economic function.

That’s exactly how the system works. That’s what it was designed for. 

---

But there’s a deeper layer here, one that concerns not just how products are sold, but how markets organize themselves when real innovation ceases to be the most profitable competitive differentiator.

In highly concentrated sectors — where three or four players dominate and entry costs are prohibitive — the incentive to truly innovate structurally drops. Genuine innovation opens markets, but it also creates vulnerability: you cannibalize your own product, take on high technical risk, and wait long cycles to see a return. In a setting where the main metric is quarterly results, this loses systematically to the safer alternative: optimize margins, invest in branding, and make the product look new without being. Gillette isn’t interested in inventing a blade that lasts ten years. The smartphone industry isn’t interested in an update cycle that makes sense to wait four years to renew.

The result is an economy where marketing has become the product. Not in a pejorative sense — in a technical sense. The competitive advantage migrated from engineering to perception. In mass-market categories — beauty, food, clothing, gadgets — the formulation itself matters less and less. What matters is the meaning the product carries, the identity it signals, the narrative around it. Red Bull didn’t invent a revolutionary drink. It invented a category of meaning.

---

For this model to scale, it relies on a quiet condition: short memory.

It’s no accident that we live in an attention-fragmented economy. The speed of launch cycles, the volume of stimuli, the architecture of algorithmic feeds — all of this isn’t a collateral effect of modern consumer capitalism. It’s a feature. A consumer with long memory is a tougher customer. They remember that the metaverse was the inevitable future in 2022. That NFTs were the new asset class in 2021. That the AirFryer was a culinary revolution, that that productivity app would transform your relationship with work, that that supplement had robust scientific evidence. The cycle is fast enough that most people don’t connect the promise to the disappointment — because there’s already a new promise occupying the same cognitive space.

Those who follow closely notice the pattern. But the system isn’t designed for those who follow closely.

---

There’s a useful distinction here, between what some economists call capture innovation and frontier innovation. The first appropriates value that already exists — reorganizes, re-packages, repositions. The second creates new value, pushes what’s possible to do. Most of what’s sold as innovation today is of the first type. The iPhone 16 compared to the 15. The new cosmetics line with the ingredient of the moment. The car model with the redesigned interface. Real increments, but sold as leaps.

Genuine frontier innovation still happens — only in places less visible and, curiously, in sectors where marketing does not solve the fundamental problem. Do you launch the rocket or not. Does gene therapy work or not. Does the language model understand context or not. In these territories — aerospace, biology, energy, artificial intelligence — real frontiers are being pushed, because the criterion of success is physical, not perceptual. The material world doesn’t negotiate with narrative.

---

The deepest irony of all this arrangement is in the device in your hand as you read this.

The smartphone is the only product that never fully disappoints, because it never becomes completely real. It reconfigures itself all the time — one more app, one more notification, one more piece of content just below the scroll. It’s the perfect anticipation machine: there’s no moment of definitive possession, no point at which the object delivers everything it promised, because the promise renews before it expires. It’s also the vehicle through which all other promises reach you. The air fryer, the online course, the supplement, the headphones that will change your relationship with music — everything passes through the same screen, with the same form factor, at the same speed.

What this suggests, for those who want to break free, is less a method than a stance. Give time to the noise. Wait for the conversation about a product to sustain itself, without campaigns, without launch cycles. Ask who talks about it after two years, and why. The products that truly change something create genuine enthusiasts — people who keep talking on their own, because the utility is real and remains. No campaign is needed to maintain a conversation about something that actually works. The conversation sustains itself.

The silence that follows the purchase isn’t neutral. It says something.
