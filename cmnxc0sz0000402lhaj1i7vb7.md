#### The 'do it without needing to know' discourse is consolidating as a transformative — and dangerous — force in contemporary development. Driven by AI adoption and a range of buzzwords, it drastically reduces execution costs and democratizes access to creations previously restricted to software engineers.

But beyond reshaping expectations about effort and understanding — that devotion that computer scientists know well — the idea of simplification corrodes something less visible and far more critical: the ability to decide clearly and to act when something goes off the rails. The problem isn't the technology itself, but how it's being adopted — replacing judgment with convenience, once again.

Cognitive debt can be understood as the accumulation of complexity that makes a system hard to understand, explain, and evolve. It isn’t in the code, but in the relationship between people and the code. And the decision-making muscle is the organizational capacity to interpret context, evaluate trade-offs, and take responsibility for choices. When this muscle stops being exercised, it atrophies. If the muscle requires continuous effort, the idea of delegated capability is fallacious. Abstraction has always been a legitimate productivity tool; the problem arises when it eliminates the need to understand without introducing equivalent control mechanisms. It’s at this point that accountability begins to dissolve.

The illusion is powerful because its momentum starts with real gains. AI tools enable faster production, testing ideas with less effort, and reduced technical barriers. Gradually, however, the role of AI stops being supportive and becomes decisional. Developers not only implement suggestions — they come to accept them as a sufficient basis to act. What previously required understanding now requires only superficial validation. The transition is subtle, but profound: assisted execution evolves into delegated decision. Largely due to the nature of this technology, whose fundamental hallmark is complacency.

There's also a little-discussed side effect in this transition: the erosion of standardization. When processes are generated by AI from situational contexts — different prompts, different moments, different people triggering the system — the result is never exactly the same. The stochastic nature of the models guarantees this: two responses can be similar, but rarely identical. In a weekly financial follow-up report, for example, this variation may seem harmless at first glance. Over time, however, it corrodes comparability, makes audits harder, and renders the decision history unreliable to reconstruct faithfully. Standardization isn't bureaucracy — it's the operational memory of an organization.

This pattern appears across multiple layers. In engineering, generated code works, but isn't understood, making maintenance and debugging high cognitive-cost activities. In product, decisions begin to be guided by AI outputs that aren't fully questioned. In operations, playbooks are followed mechanically, without adapting to new contexts. In leadership, there’s a centralization of strategic directives paired with decentralization of execution — a growing misalignment between those who set the direction and those who actually decide.

A recent case involving Amazon illustrates this pattern. After incidents tied to the use of AI agents — including autonomous actions that caused significant interruptions — the company convened engineers to reevaluate practices. What stands out isn't the error itself, but its nature: a technically valid action at the local level produced a negative systemic impact. The organizational response was to reintroduce human oversight, requiring greater supervision of AI-assisted changes. The episode isn't a deviation — it's a symptom.

As decisions come to be mediated by AI, the question "who decided?" ceases to have a clear answer. Leadership defines adoption policies, teams implement workflows, and AI executes actions. When something goes wrong, responsibility fragments. It’s not possible to attribute the error exclusively to the human, nor to the machine, nor to policy. This ambiguity isn’t merely a legal or operational problem—it’s structural, a direct result of a lack of clarity about where the decision really happens.

This scenario is fed by cognitive debt. Systems and processes become increasingly hard to understand, leading to less-informed decisions. Less-informed decisions, in turn, increase system complexity, creating a reinforcing loop. AI accelerates this cycle by enabling faster building without necessarily better understanding.

Abstraction has always promised productivity by hiding complexity. In the current context, however, it also hides the consequences. The higher the level of abstraction, the less visibility there is into the impact of decisions. This creates a paradox: tools that expand the ability to act reduce the ability to judge. Without additional control mechanisms, speed gains turn into systemic risk.

In the short term, the effect is positive: more deliveries, less friction, a greater sense of efficiency. In the medium term, inconsistencies arise, rework, and coordination difficulties. In the long term, the impact runs deeper: loss of organizational autonomy, fragility in the face of change, and growing dependence on tools and on a few individuals who still retain some real understanding of the system.

Organizations affected by this process show recurring symptoms. Systems work, but explaining them has become costly. The immediate result is hesitation to make even simple changes. Innovation, long desired, stalls. Past decisions aren’t easily justifiable. The knowledge needed to operate is scattered in informal conversations or concentrated in a few individuals who become single points of failure.

Solving this problem requires redefining expectations about the new technology. After a mental reset away from the loud hype of the last few years, it’s necessary to clearly separate who decides from who executes and stay vigilant not to slip again at this point. Establishing risk-appropriate autonomy levels and ensuring traceability of decisions have been the core of this adoption effort. More importantly: reinforce practices that develop judgment, not just tool use. The organization should offset the higher level of abstraction with commensurate gains in control and clarity. Keep the true objective in focus, constantly.

Thus, the central issue is organizational. By substituting understanding for facilitated execution, companies trade capability for convenience. This works while the environment remains stable — and fails exactly when new, ambiguous, or critical situations arise.

Delegating the rational step of the decision-making process accumulates cognitive debt, but it also atrophies teams' ability to respond to the unexpected, which, ultimately, defines their survival.
