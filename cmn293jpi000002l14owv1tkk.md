Few forces shape the fate of societies with the quiet precision of geopolitics — and of the value people assign to the things they produce and need.

Today, the topic is serious and timely.

And we’ll take as a starting point an analysis published by Investing — based on the work of Paul Donovan, UBS’s Chief Economist — about how the dollar’s dominance in global energy markets is undergoing structural reassessment.

The central argument: the economic incentives that cemented the petrodollar system are deteriorating.

But will the 2026 geopolitical landscape confirm this diagnosis — or contradict it?

Let’s walk through brief chapters, covering basically everything that has moved on the global board in recent weeks.

The petrodollar under structural pressure. Venezuela now under American tutelage. Xi Jinping without reliable generals. US debt crossing $39 trillion. The real trapped in a three-dimensional bind. And the Federal Reserve in a position nobody wants to admit aloud.

---

# The Petrodollar Hegemony Faces Its Structural Test

The historical starting point is solid.

From the 1870s to the 1950s, the United States produced more than 50% of the world’s oil and supplied the majority of extraction equipment. Producers needed dollars to settle capital expenditures. This was the fissure in the machinery — a functional necessity, not a political choice.

That fissure is eroding.

America’s share of Saudi import volumes has fallen to about 8% — less than two-thirds of what it was a decade ago. China is now the top trading partner for virtually every Gulf state. And Gulf sovereign wealth funds are increasingly directing revenue toward domestic infrastructure and non-Western weapons — rather than recycling everything into U.S. Treasuries. Varoufakis’s Global Minotaur reference is apt: deficits export dollars to the world, which recycle back via Treasuries — and it’s precisely this circuit that’s leaking.

The system ran on three simultaneous anchors.

Industrial imports into the United States, which required dollars. Purchases of American military hardware. Recycling of surpluses into Treasuries.

All three vectors are under structural pressure.

The bifurcated model Donovan highlights is the most relevant to what follows: oil would continue to be priced in dollars — but the revenue would be immediately converted into other currencies. This does not collapse the system. But it introduces chronic downward pressure on the dollar that accumulates over decades.

The erosion of the petrodollar is not a single event — it’s a process.

And slow processes are the most dangerous because they don’t trigger alarms as they advance.

# The Axis of Challenge — Russia, Iran, China, and Their Limits

The de-dollarization thesis needs actors who can execute it. Who are they in 2026, and what is their real operational condition?

Russia has built over three years a parallel settlement system — via a China-India-Turkey route, in yuan and rubles — which functioned reasonably as an escape valve. But there are serious problems: the Russian economy is under intense inflationary pressure, the ruble remains devalued, and the cost of war is draining sovereign reserves.

If Trump offers partial sanctions relief in exchange for concessions in Ukraine, Moscow has real incentives to partially reintegrate the dollar circuit.

And here’s the critical point: if Russia returns to the dollar as the settlement currency, the petro-yuan project loses its fattest supplier of cheap oil — precisely what sustained China’s room to price in yuan at a discount.

Now Iran.

The industrial-military complex has been largely degraded. Proxy warfare capacity was drastically reduced — Hezbollah degraded, Hamas operationally destroyed, Houthis contained. The Strait of Hormuz is Iran’s conventional “nuclear-weapon” lever — but it can be used once for maximum effect, and using it invites existential retaliation.

Iran as a vector for an alternative to the dollar is effectively neutralized for an indefinite period.

China is the only player with scale to build a sustainable alternative monetary system — but it’s internally weakened by purges within the command structure needed to project force. Xi purged nine of the top generals of the Communist Party, including He Weidong — the first acting vice chairman of the Central Military Commission to be removed since 1989. Most of these generals served in the military districts responsible for the Taiwan Strait.

Xi may want Taiwan. But he may not trust the army that would have to execute the operation.

The Shanghai International Energy Exchange has existed since 2018 and moves meaningful volumes with Russia and Iran. But the geopolitical fragmentation of its main suppliers limits the scale and credibility of the system as a global alternative to Brent in dollars.

# Venezuela — from Capture to Tutelage

On January 3, 2026, American military operation. Maduro captured. Transferred to New York. And last week — March 17 — Trump celebrated Venezuela’s victory over Italy in the World Baseball Classic and posted: “Good things are happening for Venezuela lately. 51st state. Anyone?”

Maduro’s capture is a modern unprecedented event: a sitting head of state was militarily extracted by a foreign power. The UN called the action a “dangerous precedent.” Trump said the United States will administer Venezuela until a transition occurs, and that American companies will pay to rebuild the country’s oil infrastructure.

This isn’t rhetoric — it’s a blueprint for economic tutelage.

And tutelage is the right word. Not occupation, not direct control — functional tutelage, built on four simultaneous pillars.

First: economic dependence. American companies like ExxonMobil, ConocoPhillips, and Halliburton will be the agents of rebuilding the oil infrastructure. Who rebuilds, controls the flows.

Second: forced political alignment. Delcy Rodríguez, interim president, was Maduro’s deputy for years — and is coordinating with Washington not out of conviction, but because the alternative is the same fate as the predecessor. It’s coercive adherence.

Third: directed oil revenue. Trump explicitly said the revenue from Venezuelan oil will pay the costs of the American operation. This is extraction of sovereign resources via tutelage — the classic template.

Fourth: the demonstrative effect. An American flag was hoisted in Caracas for the first time in seven years. The signal sent to any state considering building circuits alternative to the dollar is unambiguous.

Venezuela has the world’s largest proven oil reserves — estimated at 17% of global total, or 300 billion barrels. With American infrastructure rebuilding production capacity, this represents a massive vector for reinforcing the petrodollar circuit.

The model resembles Panama after Noriega in 1989: nominal sovereignty maintained, real control exercised via economic dependence and alignment in foreign policy.

Noriega was the 1989 template. Maduro is the 2026 update. The demonstrative effect on potential dissidents of the dollar system is calculated, not accidental.

There is a paradox, though. The same action that strengthens the dollar’s geopolitical perimeter undermines multilateralism and the framework of international norms that give credibility to American financial institutions. The IMF, the Swift system, the credibility of dollar-denominated contracts—all this depends on a perception of order, not brute force. In the long run, brute force without institutions is inflationary for American sovereign risk.

# Xi Jinping, Taiwan, and the Window That Is Closing

Taiwan remains the wildcard with the highest systemic impact. Is Xi under enough internal pressure to accelerate or delay a decision?

Internal pressure on Xi is real and documentable. The 2025 military purges removed nine senior generals, shrinking the Central Military Commission from seven to four members in two years. U.S. Defense Department assessments indicate corruption and purges have harmed readiness and trust in command.

Xi may have the intention — but may not have the reliable instrument to execute it.

The next Party Congress in 2027 will determine whether Xi secures a fourth term. Successfully seizing Taiwan would mute critics. But Xi is 72, has already broken Deng Xiaoping’s two-term norm, and his purges have created powerful enemies. The risk of a failed invasion is existential for the Chinese Communist Party — and Xi knows this.

The more sophisticated argument about Taiwan is this: the Justice Mission 2025 exercises — with 89 warplanes, drone swarms, and blockade simulations — primarily serve as an access-denial shield, not invasion rehearsals. Their strategic objective is to freeze the status quo and block American and Japanese interference. It’s a slow-strangling strategy — not D-Day.

The TSMC factor creates a clock: within two to three years, TSMC factories in Arizona, Germany, and Japan will be advanced enough to mitigate the impact of any disruption in Taiwan. The longer Xi waits, the lower the strategic value of militarily acting.

For the dollar, the most likely scenario — maximum pressure without invasion — is paradoxically stabilizing. If China enters direct confrontation with the West over Taiwan, the yuan’s internationalization project would collapse instantly. Global capital would flee to the dollar in a classic flight-to-safety, reinforcing the hegemon that is being replaced.

# The American Fiscal Crisis — the Only Enemy the U.S. Has Created on Its Own

We’ve seen that external challengers to the dollar are under pressure. But there’s a vector the United States can compromise on its own, independent of geopolitical moves: the fiscal crisis.

The numbers are, frankly, alarming.

On March 18, 2026 — this week — the national debt crossed $39 trillion for the first time, reaching that milestone in less than five months after crossing $38 trillion. An unprecedented pace of accumulation outside wars or acute crises.

The Congressional Budget Office projects: federal deficit in 2026 of $1.9 trillion. Public debt as a share of GDP in 2036: 120% — surpassing the post-World War II record of 106%. Primary deficits persist despite consolidation rhetoric. Gross public debt above 87% of GDP and on an upward trajectory. And interest payments on the debt in 2026: more than $1 trillion — nearly triple what they were in 2020.

The scariest element isn’t debt size — it’s the dynamics of interest.

Net interest payments are projected to rise from 3.2% of GDP in 2025 to 6.3% in 2054 — well beyond the previous historical peak of 3.2% in 1991. The CBO projects the average interest rate paid on the debt could exceed the economy’s growth rate starting in 2031.

If this holds, the United States slides into a self-reinforcing debt spiral: interest costs push rates higher, which suppress growth, which in turn raises costs even more. It’s the Minotaur’s supply-side turning against its own issuer.

The 2025 fiscal reconciliation law will add $500 billion to the deficit in 2026, $635 billion in 2027, and $4.1 trillion to the debt over the decade. And that on a baseline already deemed unsustainable by virtually all nonpartisan fiscal economists.

Trump’s big paradox in 2026: he simultaneously strengthens the dollar’s geopolitical perimeter — Venezuela, Iran containment, pressure on China — and destroys the fiscal foundations that undergird confidence in the currency. Two projects in different speeds. The question isn’t which is more important — it’s which wins first.

The bond market is the final arbiter. While the U.S. government can still finance itself for 30 years at 4.6%, there is residual confidence. But confidence isn’t a law of physics — it can evaporate in a quarter if circumstances change.

The United States is going bankrupt slowly. The problem is that slow doesn’t mean never. It means there’s no alarm — until there is.

And here’s an additional dimension that almost no one is modeling: the new dollar fomenters. Artificial intelligence, aerospace, and robotics could create an embryonic tech-dollar. Demand for Nvidia GPUs, cloud infrastructure, and Starlink connectivity already creates structural dollar flows. Countries need dollars to access American cognitive infrastructure — a logic that echoes the old machinery’s moat, only applied to the twenty-first century. But they’re still orders of magnitude smaller than the energy market. The low-Earth-orbit economy could be the next locus of dollar anchoring that no one is pricing yet.

# Real versus Dollar — A Currency Competing with Its Own Demerits

Okay — the dollar has problems. But for Brazil, the relevant question is different: even in a weaker-dollar scenario, does the real benefit? Or are there domestic dynamics that prevent transmission?

The trap is in the implicit premise. The real does not compete with the dollar in a league of reserve currencies. The real competes with the dollar with its own demerits — and until Brazil solves its fiscal and institutional equation, global dollar depreciation tends to be absorbed partially, not entirely, by the Brazilian currency.

The real is determined by four overlapping layers, each operating at a different frequency.

First layer: the real’s real interest-rate differential. This has historically been the strongest driver of the real in the short and medium term. Brazil operates with one of the world’s highest real interest rates. When the Selic is at 14.75% and inflation around 5%, Brazil’s real rate is an anomaly that attracts carry trades — speculative capital entering to capture the differential. The problem: this capital is inherently volatile. Any global risk-off shock triggers a simultaneous flight and a sharp depreciation of the real, regardless of Brazilian fundamentals.

Second layer: the fiscal fundamentals. The spending cap has been replaced by the fiscal framework, which has limited credibility in markets. The primary deficit persists despite consolidation rhetoric. Gross public debt is above 87% of GDP and rising. And unlike the United States, Brazil does not issue the global reserve currency — each fiscal deterioration translates directly into a currency risk premium.

Third layer: commodities — the real’s structural anchor. The real is essentially a commodity currency. With oil above $100 a barrel, Brazil benefits through two channels: Petrobras export revenues and an improved trade balance. But there’s a medium-term tension: if Venezuela is reintegrated into the American production circuit at scale — which could take 3–5 years given the state of infrastructure — this increases global oil supply and puts downward pressure on prices, which would be unfavorable for the real.

Fourth layer: political and institutional risk. The real carries a risk premium beyond fiscal concerns. Uncertainty about central bank independence, intermittently emerging heterodox narratives from the government, and the external perception that Brazil toggles between orthodox policy and fiscal populism create a floor of distrust that structurally depresses the real.

To push the real below 5 reais per dollar for a durable period would require an unlikely combination: credible fiscal consolidation, elevated commodity prices, and a weak-dollar global backdrop — all at once. To push it above 6.50 for a durable period: a severe fiscal shock or acute political crisis. The most likely scenario is a range of 5 to 6 reais per dollar, with high volatility and no clear trend.

The historical floor of distrust in the real is structural, not cyclical. The real has never returned to pre-2015 levels because the market permanently re-priced Brazil’s institutional risk after impeachment, Lava Jato, and the subsequent instability cycles.

This isn’t pessimism — it’s market memory.

# Fed, Selic, and Elections — Two Monetary Policies in Impossible Positions

In the same week: the Fed holds rates at 3.5% to 3.75% in Washington, and the Copom cuts the Selic from 15% to 14.75% in Brasília. Two simultaneous moves — or seemingly opposite — with oil at $111. What’s happening?

Starting with the Fed, because here there’s an orthodox-thesis that deserves attention. The market consensus pricing still calls for a cut in 2026 and another in 2027. But the January minutes already revealed that several participants flagged the possibility of rate hikes if inflation remains stubbornly above target. That rarely surfaces in minutes — a signal that the hawkish risk is on the table.

Powell explicitly said at the March 18 briefing: we’re balancing two objectives in a situation where labor-market risks point downward — which argues for lower rates — and inflation risks point upward. With oil at $111 and inflation projected at 2.7% for 2026, the Fed is paralyzed — not in a cutting cycle.

The most provocative angle is political. If Trump needs to show he tamed inflation before the November 2026 midterms, a Fed that raises rates now — even if painfully — could be narratively convenient. It contains inflation before the electoral cycle, and the short-term recessionary cost falls on the Fed, not on Trump. It’s the operational scapegoat thesis enacted via monetary timing.

Powell is under DOJ scrutiny, resisting a subpoena, and has signaled he won’t step down until the investigation ends. A defensive Powell has an incentive to act strictly by the data — and the data, given the oil shock, could paradoxically justify a rate hike.

In Brazil, the Copom cut — but the statement was deliberately ambiguous. What is the market reading?

The 0.25 percentage-point cut — from 15% to 14.75% — was as small as possible, and Galípolo’s briefing was surgical in its ambiguity. Some in the market read it as the start of a cycle. Others read it as a signal that the committee could pause if the scenario worsens. This ambiguity isn’t a communications error — it’s a political stance. Galípolo sits between the hammer and the anvil: inflation with oil at $111 on one side, government pressure for lower rates in an election year on the other.

According to Abicom, diesel is 47% below international prices and gasoline 42% below. The government has already suspended PIS and Cofins on diesel and proposed that states zero the ICMS on imported diesel, with the Union compensating 50% of the revenue loss. It’s fiscal sacrifice upon fiscal sacrifice — in an election year, with rising public debt. The Dilma era’s precedent is clear: price controls cost Petrobras 100 billion reais in losses — more than all Lava Jato.

The negative feedback loop for Brazil is set: if the Fed keeps rates high while the Copom cuts, the Brazil–U.S. real interest rate differential narrows. That reduces carry-trade attractiveness in the real, pushes the exchange rate higher, which raises the cost of diesel imports needed to cover Petrobras’s gap, which pressures inflation further, which constrains the Copom still more. And the election timing makes it politically hard to implement the necessary reforms before October.

Brazil is trying to do what every government does in an election year: buy time with fiscal instruments. The difference is that oil at $111 makes this game exponentially more expensive — and Petrobras’s track record says the bill always comes due.

# Synthesis — The Board in March 2026

Let’s tie it all together. What’s the state of the world in March 2026, seen through the dollar lens?

The dollar will not collapse — and probably won’t collapse in the next two decades. But the long-run trajectory points toward gradual fragmentation of the global monetary system, with multiple regional currencies gaining relevance in specific circuits. The question is timing — and the timing was set further away by a combination of factors no one anticipated unfolding in this simultaneity.

- Venezuela: under American tutelage. Strongly favorable to the dollar. Horizon 2026–2028.
- Iran: operationally neutralized. Favorable to the dollar. Immediate.
- Russia: potentially returning to the dollar circuit via negotiation. Horizon 12–18 months.
- China and Taiwan: containment without invasion — neutral, stabilizing scenario. Horizon 2027–2030.
- American fiscal crisis: structurally unfavorable to the dollar, slow but cumulative. Relevant effects from 2031 onward.
- Technology, artificial intelligence, aerospace: favorable to the dollar in an embryonic way. Horizon decades.
- Erosion of the petrodollar’s machinery in the Gulf: structurally unfavorable, very slow. Horizon decades.

The core synthesis is this: Trump is simultaneously rebuilding the dollar’s geopolitical perimeter and destroying its fiscal foundations. Two projects in different speeds. Geopolitics operates on months — captured Venezuela, Iran contained, Russia negotiating. The fiscal operates on decades — but the compounding effect of interest on a $39 trillion debt is nonlinear and can accelerate abruptly.

For Brazil, the 2026 scenario is a three-dimensional trap: high oil that should benefit Petrobras and the real, but is being captured by the government via tax breaks and price controls; Copom cutting rates with inflation above target and currency under pressure; and elections in October that make any correction politically costly. Brazil is buying time with instruments that have maturities.

The bigger irony of 2026: the country that most threatens the dollar’s long-run credibility isn’t China, Russia, or Iran. It’s the United States itself — via self-inflicted fiscal deterioration that no external foe could inflict.

The dollar survives wars. It survives sanctions. It survives substitution attempts.

The question is whether it survives arithmetic.
