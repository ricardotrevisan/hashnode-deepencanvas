*When the appearance of collaboration matters more than the explicit articulation of differences — and what that costs decisions.*

The meeting ends, the decision seems mature. Weeks later, the problems that no one mentioned — and that everyone knew — surface.

This trait is recurring: the bigger the apparent consensus, the less honest the actual discussion tends to be. A shadow of consensus forms — where the appearance of collaboration becomes more valuable than the explicit articulation of the real differences among participants.

This text names the mechanism behind this phenomenon and examines it in one of its most fertile environments: the evaluation of corporate software solutions.

The central concept
A conflationary alliance occurs when distinct interests are fused discursively under a common signifier that appears to represent everyone.

The result is a decision that seems collective, but that in practice prioritizes the interests of a subset of participants.

There are distinct interests among the actors.

A common signifier is introduced to represent everyone.

Divergence stops being discussed explicitly.

The group acts as if it were pursuing a single shared objective.

Conflation does not require conscious manipulation. It emerges from the institutional ritual itself: meetings, presentations, minutes, and approval processes create a frame that favors the appearance of convergence. Plenary meetings allow less-engaged participants to hide behind collective behavior.

The software-evaluation meeting as an arena
Corporate software procurement processes offer especially fertile ground. The intangible nature of these solutions frames decisions as technical and neutral — while in practice they involve multiple institutional interests.

Vendor — close the contract, hit sales targets

Sponsor/initiating manager — validate a decision already made, demonstrate leadership

IT — ensure security, integration, and avoid expanding the support footprint

Operations / end user — avoid adding complexity to daily work

Finance — control the total cost of ownership

HR / Legal — ensure compliance and data protection

Despite this plurality, the meeting is convened under a unifying signifier: “assess whether the tool meets the team’s needs.” The structural conflict disappears from the discourse.

“More incisive questions are interpreted as resistance or a lack of collaboration.”

The vendor’s presentation is structured to impress those with purchasing power, not those who will use the system daily. Complex IT questions receive deferred answers: “we’ll address that during implementation.” The contradiction is not resolved — it’s shifted to the future.

La Boétie in the office
In this microcosm, a mechanism described by Étienne de La Boétie in the Discourse on Voluntary Servitude appears. Power systems are not sustained by coercion alone. They endure because each level of the hierarchy agrees to cooperate with the level above in exchange for small symbolic rewards.

In corporate conflationary alliances: the manager controls career trajectories; public questioning incurs reputational costs; the meeting ritual pressures convergence at the end; and small symbolic concessions are distributed — each area is named as included by the solution.

No one is formally obliged to agree. Yet, every participant suppresses their real objection to avoid becoming the visible obstacle.

How to undo the alliance
If conflation depends on hiding differences, undoing it requires the opposite move: making interests explicit. Separate discussions by domain before plenaries, record success criteria by department, run real pilots with end users, include total cost of ownership in the analysis.

The simplest question is often the most uncomfortable:

“In what points are the interests present here actually different?”

When this question arises, the discomfort often reveals something important: the convergence that seemed natural was, in fact, the result of a conflationary alliance already in operation.
